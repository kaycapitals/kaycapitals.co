The Kay Capitals Trading System: How I Trade Options Every Day

By Somesh | Kay Capitals University | March 10, 2026

People ask me all the time: "What's your system? What do you actually do?" And I get why. There are a million traders online posting screenshots and flashy content, but very few who actually explain what they're doing under the hood.

I'm not going to give you every proprietary detail — that's what KCU is for. But I'm going to give you a real, honest look at the Kay Capitals trading system: the philosophy behind it, the principles that drive it, how I approach the market every day, and why it works for my students and me.

I'm Somesh — Kay Capitals. This is how I trade.

The Philosophy: Rules Over Opinions

The Kay Capitals trading system is built on one foundational belief: rules beat opinions, every single time.

I don't care what I think the market is going to do today. I don't care what the news says. I don't care what Twitter is screaming about. I have a set of rules. The market either triggers those rules or it doesn't. If it does, I trade. If it doesn't, I wait.

This sounds simple. It's not. Because every day, your brain will manufacture "reasons" to deviate from your rules. "Today feels different." "This news changes everything." "I just have a feeling about this one." Those feelings have cost traders more money than any bear market ever has.

The system removes opinion from the equation. It replaces "I think" with "the rules say." That shift — from subjective to objective, from feeling to framework — is what creates consistency.

What I Trade and Why

Primary instruments: SPY options, QQQ options Secondary instruments: NVDA, TSLA, AAPL, AMZN options

  • SPY and QQQ are the backbone because:
  • Massive liquidity — tight spreads, fast fills
  • Daily expirations (0DTE available every trading day)
  • Correlated with the entire market, so you're trading the biggest moves
  • Well-defined technical levels that institutions respect
  • Enough volatility for day trading, not so much that it's random

Individual names like NVDA and TSLA are for specific situations — earnings plays, outsized momentum, sector rotation days. They require more experience because they can move violently and gap without warning.

For students, I almost always start them exclusively on SPY. Master one instrument before spreading yourself across many. The system works the same way regardless of ticker — but SPY is the most forgiving place to learn it.

The Three Pillars of the System

Pillar 1: Market Structure and Context

Before I even think about a specific trade, I need to understand the environment I'm operating in. This is the "what is the market doing?" phase.

Every morning, before market open, I analyze:

The daily chart: Is SPY trending up, trending down, or range-bound? This tells me my directional bias for the day. I'm not predicting — I'm reading. If SPY has been making higher highs and higher lows for two weeks, my bias is bullish. I'm looking for call opportunities. Simple.

Key levels: Where are the major support and resistance zones? These are prices where the market has previously reversed or stalled. They're not magic lines — they're areas where supply and demand have historically shifted. I identify 2-3 key levels before the open and watch how price reacts when it approaches them.

Overnight action: What did futures do overnight? Is there a gap up or gap down? Gaps create specific trading dynamics in the first 30-60 minutes that the system accounts for.

Economic calendar: Are there Fed speakers, CPI data, jobs numbers? These events create volatility that changes how I trade. I might reduce size or sit out entirely on high-impact events.

This context analysis takes me about 15-20 minutes. By the time the bell rings, I know what I'm watching and what conditions would trigger a trade. There's no scrambling at the open.

Pillar 2: The Setup Criteria

This is the "when do I trade?" part. And it's where the system's rules are most specific.

I can't give you the exact entry criteria here — that would undermine the value of the mentorship and the years of development behind it. But I can give you the principles:

Price must come to a level I've pre-identified. I don't chase. I don't enter mid-move. I wait for price to reach a zone I've marked before the open. This alone eliminates 70% of impulsive trades.

There must be a reaction at the level. Price hitting a level isn't enough. I need to see HOW it hits the level. Is there rejection? Absorption? Momentum continuation? The character of the move at the level tells me whether to engage.

Volume must confirm. I want to see volume supporting the move. Low-volume moves at key levels are often traps. High-volume reactions give me higher confidence that the move is legitimate.

The risk-to-reward must be at least 2:1. Before entering, I know my stop and my target. If the potential reward isn't at least twice my risk, the trade doesn't qualify. Period. This risk management principle is non-negotiable.

The setup must match the daily bias. If my morning analysis says bullish bias and I see a bullish setup at a support level with volume — that's alignment. If I see a bullish setup in a bearish-biased environment — that's a lower-probability trade that I'm more likely to skip.

When all criteria align, I enter. When they don't, I wait. Some days that means 3-4 trades. Some days it means zero. The system doesn't care about your desire to trade. It only cares about whether conditions are met.

Pillar 3: Execution and Management

This is the "how do I handle the trade?" part, and it's where most systems fall apart because they don't address what happens AFTER you click buy.

Entry execution: When the setup triggers, I enter. No hesitation, no "let me wait for one more confirmation." Criteria met = enter. The psychology here is critical — hesitation is expensive in options because premiums move fast.

Stop loss placement: My stop goes at the level where my thesis is invalidated. Not based on how much I'm "willing to lose" emotionally, but based on where the setup objectively fails. If I'm buying at support, my stop is below that support. Logical. Structure-based.

Profit target management: This is where the art meets the science. I have a primary target (the minimum I'm aiming for — the 2:1 reward) and an extended target for when the move really runs. I typically take partial profits at the first target and let the remainder run with a trailing stop.

  • Trade management rules:
  • If the trade goes to breakeven quickly, I move my stop to breakeven. Free trade.
  • If the trade goes to my first target, I take 50-75% off and trail the rest.
  • If the trade sits there and does nothing for 15-20 minutes, I evaluate the time stop. Am I tying up capital in a dead trade?
  • If major news hits while I'm in a position, I reduce size immediately and reassess.

Exit execution: When my stop is hit, I exit. No hoping. No praying. No "let me give it a little more room." The stop is the stop. This is where journaling data proves its worth — students who track their results see clearly that every time they moved a stop further away, it cost them.

What Makes This System Different

I'll be blunt — there's nothing revolutionary about price action, support/resistance, and volume. These concepts have existed since markets began. So what makes the Kay Capitals trading system different?

Integration. It's not just a setup — it's a complete framework that integrates technical analysis, risk management, and psychological protocols into one cohesive system. Most traders have fragments of each. They know some TA, they've read about risk management, they vaguely understand psychology. But they've never had all three woven into a single, step-by-step operating system for every trading day.

Specificity. The rules are specific. Not "look for support and buy there" — specific criteria for what qualifies as a valid setup, how to enter, where to place stops, when to take profits. This specificity removes ambiguity, and ambiguity is where emotional trading thrives.

Adaptability. The system works in trending markets and range-bound markets. It works on green days and red days. It works on SPY, QQQ, NVDA, and TSLA. The principles are universal — the application adjusts to conditions. Part of the mentorship is learning when conditions favor which approach.

Focus on consistency over home runs. This system is designed for steady, repeatable execution — not to hit 10x trades. The goal is small, controlled gains with managed risk, not home runs. The compound effect of consistency is where long-term growth comes from. Individual results vary and there are no guarantees of specific returns.

A Day in the Life: How This Plays Out

Let me walk you through a typical trading day to make this concrete:

7:00 AM (Pre-market): I pull up the SPY daily chart. We've been in an uptrend, making higher highs. Bias is bullish. I mark yesterday's high, yesterday's low, and a clean support level at a prior consolidation zone. I check the economic calendar — nothing major today. Plan is set.

9:30 AM (Market Open): SPY opens with a small gap up. I don't chase the gap. I note where price is relative to my levels and wait.

9:45 AM: SPY pulls back to my identified support level. I'm watching closely. Volume picks up. I see a rejection candle forming at the level. The setup criteria are approaching alignment.

9:52 AM: Criteria met. I buy SPY calls. Stop below the support level. Target at the next resistance zone — 2.5:1 reward-to-risk. Position sized at 1% of account.

10:10 AM: Trade moves in my favor. Reaching the first target. I take 60% off, move stop to breakeven on the remainder.

10:30 AM: Remainder hits the extended target. I close the trade. A solid win within my risk parameters.

10:45 AM-12:00 PM: Nothing else qualifies. SPY is chopping in a tight range between my levels. No criteria met = no trades. I wait.

12:30 PM: Another potential setup forms. Criteria partially met but volume isn't there. Skip.

1:30 PM: A clean setup triggers on a QQQ pullback. I take it. Manages to my target level. Another controlled win.

2:30 PM: Daily plan is executed. Two trades, both green. I'm done for the day. Could there be another setup in the last hour? Sure. But I've hit my daily goal and I don't need to push.

That's it. Not glamorous. Not exciting. But it works. Day after day, week after week.
