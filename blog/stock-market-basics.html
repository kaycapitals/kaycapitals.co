Stock Market Basics for Beginners: Everything You Need to Know Before You Trade

By Somesh | Kay Capitals University

If you're brand new to the stock market and you have no idea where to start — good. You're in the right place. Most people skip this step entirely. They throw money at random tickers, lose a chunk of their account, and then wonder why trading "doesn't work." It works. You just didn't learn the basics first.

I'm Somesh — a full-time day trader and the founder of Kay Capitals University. I've been in this game for years, and the number one thing I see from beginners is they jump in without understanding what the stock market even is. So let's fix that right now. After reading this, check out our first year trading tips to build the right foundation.

What Is the Stock Market, Really?

Think of the stock market like any other market. You go to a grocery store to buy apples and bananas. The stock market is where you go to buy shares of companies — Amazon, Tesla, AMD, Apple, whatever you want.

When you buy a share of a company, you're buying a small piece of ownership in that company. Shares are also called equities. You might also hear people talk about options and derivatives — those are different instruments you can trade, and we'll get into options trading in other posts. But at its core, the stock market is just a marketplace where buyers and sellers come together. Before you start, also read about the most common day trading mistakes to avoid costly pitfalls.

What You Need to Get Started

To buy and sell stocks, you need a broker account. That's your gateway into the market. There are plenty of brokers out there — Interactive Brokers, TradeStation, Thinkorswim, Webull — the list goes on.

I've personally used most of them, and the one I keep coming back to is Interactive Brokers. It's clean, it's powerful, and it does what I need it to do. But pick whichever one works for you. The important thing is you actually open an account and learn the platform before you start throwing money around.

Two Types of Trading You Need to Know

When it comes to how to start trading stocks, there are two main approaches: fundamental analysis and technical analysis.

Fundamental Analysis

This is where you study the company itself — earnings reports, balance sheets, revenue growth, the business model. You're looking at whether the company is a good long-term investment. People who bought Apple at $50 a share and held it? That's fundamental analysis paying off.

If you want to learn more about fundamentals, Investopedia is a great free resource. But that's not where I spend most of my time.

Technical Analysis

Technical analysis is what I do as a day trader. You're not looking at the company's financials — you're looking at the chart. Price action. Patterns. Levels. It's all about reading what the market is telling you through price movement. For a deep dive into this approach, check out our complete price action trading course.

Within technical analysis, there are two main styles:

  • Price action trading — reading raw price movement on the chart
  • Indicator-based trading — using tools like moving averages, RSI, MACD, etc.

I'm a price action guy. Sometimes I'll use indicators, but 90% of the time I'm watching raw price. It keeps things simple, and simple works.

How Much Money Do You Need to Start?

This is the question everyone asks. My honest answer: $3,000.

Why? Because $3,000 gives you enough runway to actually learn. You can make mistakes — and you will make mistakes — without blowing your account in the first week. It keeps you afloat for about six months or more while you figure out how you react to the market, how you handle losses, and how you manage risk.

The goal with your first $3,000 isn't to get rich. The goal is to learn. Focus on not losing money. Focus on understanding yourself as a trader.

How Long Does It Take to Become Profitable?

Real talk: six months to two years. That's the general consensus, and it lines up with what I've seen from students at Kay Capitals University.

But here's the catch — that timeline only applies if you're actually putting in the work. That means:

  • Having a trading plan
  • Following trading rules
  • Learning risk management
  • Studying your trades
  • Building a system

If you're just winging it, buying random stocks because someone on Reddit said so, you'll be in the red for a lot longer than two years.

The Mindset Shift Most Beginners Miss

Here's what separates the people who make it from the people who don't: the ones who make it treat this like a skill, not a lottery ticket.

You wouldn't walk into a hospital and start performing surgery because you watched a YouTube video. Trading is no different. It's a skill that takes time, practice, and discipline to develop.

The stock market will humble you. It doesn't care about your feelings, your rent, or your dreams. It only responds to preparation and execution. So prepare. Execute. And keep showing up.
