How to Make High-Quality Trading Decisions (And Stop Losing Money to Bad Ones)

By Somesh | Kay Capitals University

Every single day, you make hundreds of decisions. Most of them are invisible — what to eat, when to leave, whether to open that water bottle. But in day trading, your decisions have a price tag attached. A good decision makes you money. A bad one takes it.

The problem isn't that you make bad decisions. The problem is you don't have a system for making good ones. I'm Somesh, founder of Kay Capitals University, and improving my trading decision making is the single biggest thing that changed my results. Let me show you how.

Why Most Trading Decisions Are Terrible

Decision making is subjective. The only way you know if a decision was good or bad is by trying it, experiencing the result, and learning from it. That's how the brain works.

Think about it: if a dog bit you as a kid, you'd be cautious around dogs for the rest of your life. That's your brain using past experience to shape future decisions. Totally rational.

The same thing happens in trading. You take a loss using a particular strategy, and your brain says: "That strategy is dangerous. Don't use it again." But here's the trap — sometimes the loss had nothing to do with the strategy. Maybe you didn't follow your stop loss. Maybe you sized too big. Maybe it was just a normal loss in a system that wins 70% of the time.

My $30,000 Lesson

I took a $30,000 hit on a trade once. It was devastating. And my brain immediately blamed the strategy.

For weeks after, every time I saw the same setup, I hesitated. I couldn't execute. The fear from that one loss made me miss trade after trade — all of which would have been profitable. The money I lost from NOT trading was way more than the original $30K.

Then I sat down and reviewed the journal. You know what I found? The strategy wasn't the problem. I was. I didn't respect my stop loss on that trade. That was the actual mistake. But I'd been blaming the wrong thing for weeks.

The Upgrade Loop: How to Make Better Trades

Here's the framework I use and teach at Kay Capitals University:

Step 1: Identify the Root Cause

When a trade goes wrong, don't just feel bad about it. Dig into why. Was it the strategy? The entry? The stop loss? The position size? Your emotional state? Most of the time, it's not what you think. Journaling is the only way to see the real pattern.

Step 2: Learn the Lesson

Once you find the root cause, extract the lesson. Not "this strategy sucks" — that's lazy. The real lesson might be "I need to respect my stop loss" or "I need to wait for confirmation before entering."

Step 3: Upgrade Your System

Take that lesson and implement it. Change your rules. Adjust your checklist. This is what I mean by "upgrading your software." Your brain is the operating system — every lesson is a patch.

Step 4: Execute with the Upgraded System

Go trade with your new rules. See what happens. If you make another mistake — great. Go back to Step 1. This loop never ends. It's how you grow as a trader.

Try → Learn → Upgrade → Execute → Repeat.

Reduce Decision Fatigue Before the Market Opens

Here's something most traders don't think about: you only have so many good decisions in a day.

Every decision you make during the trading session drains your mental battery. By the time you've made 20 decisions, the quality of decision #21 is garbage. This is called decision fatigue, and it's a silent account killer.

The Fix: Pre-Market Planning

Make as many decisions as possible before the market opens. Here's what your pre-market routine should cover:

  • Which stocks you're watching
  • Key support and resistance levels
  • Your entry criteria
  • Stop loss levels
  • Profit targets
  • Which options you'll trade (strike, expiration)
  • Position size

When you do this, you eliminate five or six real-time decisions that would have drained your focus. This takes about 60 minutes before the bell. And then your actual trading session might only be 30-60 minutes of execution.

Let Go of Past Losses

You have to stop being married to your past. The monetary loss? You deserved it. Not as punishment — as tuition. If you're trying to learn day trading in a market where 95% of participants lose money, losses are the cost of education. Accept that, and the emotional weight disappears.

The Goal: Fewer, Better Decisions

The best traders I know don't make more decisions than everyone else. They make fewer, better decisions. They have a plan. They know their levels. They know their criteria. When the setup appears, they execute. When it doesn't, they sit on their hands.
