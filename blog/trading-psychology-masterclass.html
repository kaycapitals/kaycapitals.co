Trading Psychology Masterclass: How to Stop Your Brain From Destroying Your Trades

By Somesh | Kay Capitals University

You've heard the saying: trading is 80% psychology, 20% skill. I have a different take. I believe it's 80% system, 20% psychology — because a good system already covers most of the psychology for you. It tells you when to buy, when to sell, what to do.

Psychology only becomes a problem when you start thinking. The moment you start second-guessing, overthinking, or trying to outsmart your own system — that's when everything falls apart.

I'm Somesh, founder of Kay Capitals University and full-time day trader. This is my trading psychology course — the deep dive into every mental trap that destroys traders, and exactly how to escape them.

You Are Not the Problem — Until You Start Thinking

Think back to your worst trading days. Were they the days where you saw a setup, executed cleanly, and got in and out? Or were they the days you sat there for 20 minutes overthinking whether to pull the trigger?

It's always the overthinking days. Always.

When you overthink before you execute, you freeze. Or worse — you force a trade that doesn't fit your system because you've been staring at the screen for two hours and feel like you "need" to trade.

How to Stay Busy During Market Hours

If overthinking is the killer, the antidote is staying busy. Not busy trading — busy doing other things between setups.

Here's what I do: I play chess on my phone. I watch a show on my iPad. I stay engaged with my Discord community where we keep the energy light and fun. The point is to avoid sitting in silence staring at candles, because that's when your brain starts creating problems that don't exist.

The Equity Curve Every Struggling Trader Recognizes

If you're struggling with psychology, your equity curve probably looks like this: you grind your account up slowly, then give it all back in one or two emotional sessions. Up, up, up — crash. Up, up — crash.

There's always a psychological number where it breaks. Maybe your account started at $2K. You grind it to $5K. And every single time you get near $5K, you blow up. It's become a mental bottleneck.

The Hack: Add Money to Break the Ceiling

When I hit these ceilings, I just added a few hundred bucks to my account. Boom — I'm over the number. The psychological hurdle isn't a hurdle anymore. My brain stops panicking because it's no longer at "that number."

It sounds almost too simple, but it works. You're tricking your brain into moving past the barrier so you can focus on trading, not on your account balance.

When to Walk Away: The Three-Strike Rule

One of the hardest things in trading is knowing when to stop. Here's my framework:

Three consecutive losing trades = I'm done for the day.

Not three losses total — three in a row. And I start each day with a small position size. If my first trade is red, my second trade is even smaller. I'm not trying to recoup. I'm trying to set the tone.

If I hit three losses in a row, I close the broker. Literally. I press the X. I'm done. Why? Because every single time I've broken this rule and kept trading, the pain was so devastating — emotionally, mentally, financially — that I never want to feel it again.

Stop Trading Your P&L

Here's the key: I don't look at my P&L while I'm trading. I removed the P&L tab from my broker years ago. I have no idea if I'm up or down on the day while I'm in the session.

Why? Because the moment you see a number — "+$2,800" or "-$2,800" — you start trading the number instead of trading your system. You take dumb plays to get back to green. You cut winners short because you don't want to "give back" profit.

Check your P&L on your phone after market close. Deal with the emotions then. Don't bring them into live trading.

How to Respect Your Stop Loss

Having a stop loss and actually respecting it are two completely different things. Most traders know where their stop loss should be. They just can't pull the trigger when price gets there.

Why? Too many levels on your chart.

You entered on a 2-minute chart. Price hits your stop. But then you check the 5-minute — "oh, there's a 200 moving average here." You check the 10-minute — "oh, there's a support level." You start looking for confirmation bias. You're looking for any reason to stay in the trade, just like staying in a toxic relationship.

The Fix: Simplify Everything

  • 9:30 to 10:00 — Only look at the 2-minute chart
  • 10:00 to 11:00 — Only look at the 5-minute chart
  • 11:00 onward — Only look at the 10-minute chart

One time frame. That's it. No switching back and forth looking for reasons to stay in a losing trade. If your stop gets hit on your time frame, you're out. You can always re-enter later.

Fear and Greed: The Venn Diagram of Profit

Don't try to eliminate fear and greed. You can't — and you shouldn't.

Fear keeps you from being reckless. Without it, you'll oversize and blow up.

Greed keeps your runners running. Without it, you'll scalp 10% and miss the 200% move.

The balance is negative risk management: take 50% off at your first target. Move your stop to break even. Let the runner ride. You've locked in profit (fear satisfied) and you're still in for the bigger move (greed satisfied).

Missed Trades: Let Them Go

When you miss a trade, it hurts more than a loss. Why? Because you can't blame anyone else. It's purely on you.

Here's how I handle it: I journal it. I write down why I missed it. Was I distracted? Was I scared to execute? Was I answering Discord messages instead of watching my charts?

Over time, you see patterns. For me, about 40% of my missed trades happen because I'm answering student questions during market hours. That's a real data point I can act on.

But in the moment? I tell myself: "I'll deal with this when I'm journaling. Right now, focus on the next setup." Don't let a missed trade turn into a revenge trade.

The Dirty Dishes Principle

Here's something weird I do: when I wash dishes, I leave two or three spoons in the sink on purpose. When I clean my whiteboard, I leave a few marks.

Why? Because I'm training my brain to accept imperfection. Perfection doesn't exist in trading. If you're the type of person who needs everything to be clean, organized, and perfect before you can function — you're going to struggle with trading.

Markets are messy. Entries are never textbook. Stop losses get hit by 3 cents before reversing. You need to be okay with that. How you handle imperfection outside trading is exactly how you'll handle it inside trading.

Breaking a Losing Streak

When I had a brutal 13-day losing streak, I didn't try to make it all back in one day. I came in the next day with tiny size and one goal: break the chain.

I made $90 that day. Ninety dollars. And I closed my broker immediately. The chain was broken. The next day, my mindset completely shifted from "I need to recover" to "the streak is over, let's trade."

And then I made all 13 days of losses back in a single day.

If you're in a losing streak: stop trying to recover. Just break the chain. Walk away green with $20 if you have to. The psychological reset is worth more than any dollar amount.

Journal Everything — Especially the Trades You Miss

Your journal is not just for winners and losers. It's for:

  • Trades you missed (and why)
  • Days you broke your rules (and what happened)
  • Emotional states when you traded
  • Patterns in your behavior

Over time, your journal becomes a mirror. It shows you exactly who you are as a trader — not who you think you are. And that self-awareness is the foundation of every master trading psychology shift you'll ever make.
