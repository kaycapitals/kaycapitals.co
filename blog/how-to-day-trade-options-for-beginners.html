What Are Options (and Why Trade Them)?

If you don't understand what an option actually is, nothing else matters. So let's start here.

An option is a contract that gives you the right to buy or sell a stock at a specific price by a specific date. There are two types:

  • Call options: You profit when the stock goes UP
  • Put options: You profit when the stock goes DOWN

Why trade options instead of just buying stocks? Leverage. With options, you can control the movement of 100 shares of stock for a fraction of the cost. A $500 move in SPY stock might require $50,000+ in capital to capture by buying shares. With options, you might capture that same move with $500-$2,000.

But here's what nobody tells beginners: that leverage works both ways. You can make money fast, and you can lose money even faster. Options can go to zero. Your entire investment in a trade can disappear. That's why risk management isn't optional — it's survival.

The Instruments: What to Actually Trade

As a beginner, you don't need to trade 50 different stocks. You need to master a few high-liquidity names. Here's where I recommend starting:

SPY (S&P 500 ETF): This is the gold standard for options day trading. Massive volume, tight spreads, predictable patterns. If you only ever traded SPY options, you could build a full-time career from it. This is where most KCU students start and where many stay.

QQQ (Nasdaq 100 ETF): Similar to SPY but more tech-weighted. Moves a bit more aggressively. Great for traders who like slightly higher volatility.

NVDA, TSLA, AAPL, AMZN: Individual stock options with high volume and big moves. More advanced, but valuable once you have your foundation. NVDA and TSLA in particular can have monster moves in a single session.

Start with SPY. I'm serious. Don't spread yourself thin across ten tickers. Master one, then expand.

Understanding the Basics: What You Need to Know Before You Trade

The Greeks — Simplified

Every options guide throws the Greeks at you like a textbook. Here's what actually matters for day trading:

Delta: How much your option moves for every $1 the stock moves. A delta of 0.50 means your option gains or loses $0.50 for every $1 move in the stock. For day trading, I generally trade options with delta between 0.30-0.60.

Theta (Time Decay): Your option loses value every day just from time passing. The closer to expiration, the faster it decays. This is why holding options overnight as a beginner is risky as hell.

Implied Volatility (IV): High IV = expensive options. Low IV = cheap options. You generally want to buy options when IV is reasonable, not when it's spiked from a news event.

Gamma: How fast delta changes. Higher gamma means your option's price can accelerate quickly — great when you're right, devastating when you're wrong.

You don't need a PhD in the Greeks. You need a working understanding of how they affect your trades. That's enough to start.

Expiration Dates

This is where beginners get destroyed. Options expire. Unlike stocks, they don't just sit in your account waiting.

  • 0DTE (Zero Days to Expiration): Options that expire the same day you trade them. SPY and QQQ have daily expirations. These are extremely popular for day trading because the moves are fast and the premiums are lower. But the risk of losing 100% of your trade is real. I trade 0DTE regularly, but I have years of experience and strict rules around them.
  • 1-5 DTE: Options expiring within the week. Gives you a little more time but still aggressive. Good for day trades where you want slightly less theta pressure.
  • Weekly/Monthly: Longer-dated options. Less affected by time decay but more expensive. Better for swing trades than day trades.

My recommendation for beginners: Start with 1-3 DTE options on SPY. They give you enough time that a temporary pullback won't automatically kill your trade, but they're still responsive enough for day trading. Graduate to 0DTE once you have consistent execution.

Setting Up to Trade

Your Broker

You need a broker that supports options trading with fast execution. I'm not going to recommend specific brokers because they change, but here's what matters:

  • Fast fill speeds (this matters more than you think)
  • Reasonable options commissions
  • A clean options chain view
  • The ability to set stop losses on options positions
  • Mobile app that's reliable (for emergency management)

Your Account Size

"How much money do I need to start?" I get this question every day.

The honest answer: you can start learning with as little as $2,000-$5,000. You won't get rich, but you can learn the mechanics with real money at real risk.

Pattern Day Trader (PDT) Rule: If your account is under $25,000, you're limited to 3 day trades per 5 business days. This actually isn't the worst thing for beginners because it forces you to be selective. Some brokers have workarounds (cash accounts, etc.), but understand this rule exists.

The ideal starting account for serious day trading is $25,000+ to avoid PDT restrictions. But don't let the number stop you from starting. Trade within the rules, learn the skill, and scale up.

Your Trading Setup

  • You don't need six monitors and a $5,000 computer. You need:
  • One reliable screen (a laptop works fine)
  • A solid internet connection
  • Your broker platform open
  • A chart of what you're trading
  • A journal (spreadsheet, notebook, whatever)

That's it. I've seen students trade profitably from a laptop on their kitchen table. The setup doesn't make the trader.

Your First Trades: A Step-by-Step Framework

Here's how I'd approach my first day trades if I were starting from scratch:

Step 1: Watch Before You Trade

Spend at least 1-2 weeks just watching SPY with its options chain open. Don't trade. Just watch how price moves, how options prices respond, how the open differs from midday, how the last hour behaves. Build pattern recognition before you risk a dollar.

Step 2: Define Your Risk Before Every Trade

  • Before you enter any trade, you should know:
  • Exactly how much money you're willing to lose on this trade
  • Where your stop loss is
  • What your profit target is
  • Why you're entering (what's the setup?)

If you can't answer all four, you don't take the trade. Period.

Step 3: Start Small — Embarrassingly Small

Your first real trades should be 1 contract. One. I don't care if your account is $50,000. Trade 1 contract until you can execute consistently for 2-4 weeks. Then add a contract. This isn't about making money yet — it's about building habits.

Step 4: Journal Everything

  • After every trade, write down:
  • What you traded and why
  • Your entry price, exit price, stop loss, target
  • What happened
  • What you did right
  • What you did wrong
  • How you felt during the trade

That last one is more important than you think. Trading psychology starts with awareness, and awareness starts with journaling.

Step 5: Review Weekly

Every weekend, review your trades. Look for patterns — not in the market, but in yourself. Do you always lose money on Mondays? Do you overtrade after a big win? Do you hold losers too long? The data tells the story. Your job is to listen.

The Mistakes Every Beginner Makes (And How to Avoid Them)

I've mentored many beginners through KCU. These are the mistakes I see over and over:

Mistake 1: Trading too big, too fast. You're not going to make $10,000 your first month. Trying to will end your trading career before it starts. Start small, build the skill, then scale.

Mistake 2: No stop losses. "I'll just watch it and get out if it goes against me." No, you won't. You'll freeze. You'll hope. You'll pray. And the loss will be ten times what it should have been. Use stop losses. Always.

Mistake 3: Overtrading. More trades ≠ more money. In fact, the opposite is usually true. Quality over quantity. 2-3 good trades beat 15 mediocre ones every time.

Mistake 4: Chasing. SPY just ran up $3 in 10 minutes and you think "I need to get in!" No. You missed it. The next setup will come. Chasing is how you buy the top and sell the bottom.

Mistake 5: Ignoring the bigger picture. You see a bullish 5-minute candle and buy calls without realizing SPY has been in a downtrend all day on the hourly chart. Always know the bigger picture context before entering a trade.

Mistake 6: No trading plan. Trading without a plan is gambling. Your plan defines when you trade, what you trade, how much you risk, and your rules for entry and exit. Write it down. Follow it.

The Realistic Timeline

Here's what most people won't tell you: becoming a consistently profitable options trader takes time. Not weeks. Months.

  • Month 1-2: You're learning the mechanics. Expect small losses. This is tuition.
  • Month 3-4: You start seeing patterns in your own behavior. Execution improves. Still probably not consistently profitable.
  • Month 5-6: If you've been disciplined, consistent, and learning from your mistakes, this is where it starts to click. Consistency begins to emerge.
  • Month 6-12: You're refining. The system works, you're just getting better at executing it. Profits start to feel real and repeatable.

Is this timeline faster with a mentor? Yes. Dramatically. But even with the best mentorship in the world, you're still putting in the reps. There's no shortcut to screen time.

What Separates Day Trading Options From Everything Else

Day trading options is, in my biased but informed opinion, one of the most powerful income skills you can develop. Here's why:

  • Income potential scales with skill. Unlike a salary, your trading income can grow as your skill and account size develop — though losses can scale too.
  • Location independent. You need a laptop and internet. That's it.
  • Time efficient. I trade for 2-4 hours during market hours. The rest of my day is mine.
  • Compounding potential. Consistent gains, even small ones, can compound meaningfully over time — but this requires sustained discipline and there are no guarantees of any specific return.

But it's also one of the hardest skills to develop. The market doesn't care about your feelings, your bills, or your goals. It moves how it moves, and your job is to adapt. That difficulty is what makes the skill so valuable — most people can't do it, which is exactly why it pays so well for those who can.
