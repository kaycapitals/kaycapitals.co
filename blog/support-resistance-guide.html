How to Draw Support and Resistance Levels: A Day Trader's Step-by-Step Guide

By Somesh | Kay Capitals University

If you're day trading and you don't know how to draw support and resistance levels, you're flying blind. You don't know where to enter. You don't know where to take profits. You don't know where to set your stop loss. You're just... guessing.

Support and resistance trading is the foundation of everything I do. It's the first thing I teach at Kay Capitals University because without it, nothing else works.

What Are Support and Resistance Levels?

Think of support as the floor and resistance as the ceiling.

Support = The Floor

Support is a price level where the stock keeps bouncing. Every time price drops to that level, buyers step in and push it back up. When a resistance level breaks and price moves above it, that old resistance becomes the new support. The ceiling becomes the floor.

Resistance = The Ceiling

Resistance is the opposite. It's the level where price keeps getting rejected. Price goes up, hits resistance, drops back down. Like throwing a ball at the ceiling — it always comes back.

How to Spot Support and Resistance Levels

It's all about observing where price has bounced or been rejected in the past. Here's my exact process:

Step 1: Start on the Daily Chart

I always start on the daily chart to understand the bigger picture. Switch to a line chart — not candlesticks. The line chart smooths everything out and makes it way easier to see where price has been turning around.

Step 2: Move to the 60-Minute Chart

This is where I draw most of my levels. The hourly chart gives you enough detail to find actionable levels without drowning in noise.

Pro tip: Turn on extended trading hours. Pre-market and after-hours price action reveals levels you'd never see otherwise.

On the 60-minute line chart, draw lines wherever price clearly turned around: Where did it bounce hard? Where did it get rejected multiple times? Where did it consolidate before a big move?

Step 3: Adjust to Clean Numbers

Once you've drawn your levels, adjust them to whole numbers or clean half-dollar increments. If your level sits at $193.47, round it to $193.50. If it's at $191.87, make it $192.

Why? Algorithms love whole numbers. A huge amount of trading volume comes from algos, and they're programmed around round numbers.

Step 4: Drop to the 5-Minute Chart for Trading

Now you've got your levels drawn. Go to the 5-minute chart — this is where the action happens. Your levels from the hourly chart are already plotted. Now you just need a plan:

  • If price breaks above resistance → go long toward the next level
  • If price breaks below support → go short toward the next level

Two Critical Rules for Support and Resistance Trading

Rule 1: It's a Zone, Not a Line

Your lines don't have to be perfect. Support and resistance is about areas, not exact prices. Don't obsess over getting it pixel-perfect. Get in the right area and trade accordingly.

Rule 2: The More Touches, the More Important the Level

Think of a freshly frozen lake. One person stands on it — probably fine. Ten people bounce on the same spot? Eventually it cracks. The more times price tests a level, the more significant it becomes. But also — the more it's tested, the more likely it is to eventually break.

A Real Example: Trading Apple with Levels

  1. Drew levels on the daily chart — identified the overall uptrend
  2. Moved to the 60-minute chart — found key support at $192 and $191.50, resistance at $193 and $193.50
  3. Adjusted to clean numbers
  4. Went to the 5-minute chart with a plan: Below $192.50 → short to $192, then $191.50. Above $193 → long to $193.50

Market opens. Price stays between levels — we stay cash. No setup, no trade. That's patience. Learning when to trade and when to stay out is crucial - read our guide on reading market conditions to improve your timing.

Then $192.50 breaks. Short entry. First target $192 — hit. Second target $191.50 — hit. Later, $193 breaks. Long entry. Target $193.50 — hit.

Every single move respected the levels we drew before the market opened. This systematic approach to level identification works particularly well with options strategies - see how levels integrate with our 3-step options strategy.

Make This Part of Your Daily Routine

  1. Daily chart — check the trend
  2. Hourly chart — draw levels
  3. Adjust levels to clean numbers
  4. 5-minute chart — trade the plan
