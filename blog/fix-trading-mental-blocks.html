How to Fix Your Mind So Day Trading Actually Becomes Easy

By Somesh | Kay Capitals University

If you're trading options and your emotions take over the moment you see $100 in profit — or the second a pullback happens and you panic sell — then this is the trading mindset reset you've been looking for.

After 8 years of day trading, I've learned one thing that separates profitable traders from everyone else: most traders don't struggle with finding entries. They struggle with holding runners and controlling their emotions. These patterns are deeply rooted in psychology, which is why understanding trading psychology is essential for breaking through mental barriers.

I'm Somesh, founder of Kay Capitals University, and I'm about to break down the exact four methods I use to hold my winners, stop panic selling, and make bigger money — especially on small accounts.

Why Small Accounts Need Big Moves

Let's be real. If you're trading with a $300 position size and you catch a 10% move, that's $30. After commissions, market data fees, and subscriptions — you barely break even.

Small accounts need bigger moves. And big moves only come when you let your runners run. You can't panic every time your contracts drop from $150 to $140 for 20 seconds. That's not risk management — that's fear management. And fear is costing you money. If you find yourself hesitating to execute trades or cutting winners too early, read our guide on overcoming fear of execution for specific tactics to build confidence.

Rule 1: Hide Your P&L

This is the big one. Stop looking at your profit and loss while you're trading.

Every time you glance at your P&L, you trigger emotions. "Oh my God, I was up $400 a minute ago. Now I'm only up $250. What if I lose it all? Let me get out."

That panic only happens because you can see the money. On Interactive Brokers — the broker I use — I literally removed the P&L tab from my screen years ago. I don't know if I'm up or down on any trade. And because I don't know, I'm not scared of losing something — which automatically makes me hold my winners longer.

If you can do this one thing — hide your P&L — your emotional mistakes will drop instantly.

Rule 2: The Break-Even Runner System

This rule is mandatory if you trade multiple contracts. Here's how it works:

  1. Enter with at least two contracts (say, at $120 each)
  2. Price hits your first profit target — contracts are at $150
  3. Sell one contract at $150 (locked in $30 profit)
  4. Immediately set a break-even stop loss on your second contract at $120

Now your runner is at zero risk. If the stock reverses, you don't lose money on the runner. You already banked profit from the first contract. And the runner either hits break even or your next profit target.

This is negative risk management. It frees your mind completely. You're not worried about giving back profit because the worst case is already covered.

This single rule has helped me hold big trending moves for years.

Rule 3: Trail Your Stop Loss on the Stock — Not the Option

This is the rule that separates beginners from traders who actually grow their accounts.

Most traders set their stop loss based on how their option premium looks. "I'm up 30% on the contract, I don't want to give it back." That's emotional. That's not a system.

Instead, base your stop loss on the stock price structure. If the stock is in an uptrend — making higher highs and higher lows — every time a new higher low forms, you move your stop up. Not based on your feelings. Not based on the option premium. Based on actual market structure.

On trending days, this is how I catch $1, $5, $10, $15, even $20 moves. Because instead of scalping 10% and running, I let the market tell me when the trend is over by breaking structure.

Rule 4: Remove the Bids and Asks From Your Option Chain

Even after hiding my P&L, my brain would still quickly calculate my profit from the bid-ask prices showing on screen. "OK, I got in at $1.20 and the bid is now $1.50, so I'm up $30 per contract..."

So I removed them. I took the bid and ask prices completely off my option chain display.

Now I can't see my entry. I can't see my current premium. I don't know if I'm up or down. I trade purely off the stock price — which is what actually matters.

When you stop seeing premiums jump around, you stop panicking. You stop micromanaging every candle. You stop exiting too early. You start focusing on levels, price action, and structure — not emotional noise.

Why This Works: Eliminating Emotional Triggers

The hardest thing in trading isn't finding setups. It's:

  1. Letting your runners run
  2. Not sabotaging yourself while in profit

When you hide your P&L, set break-even stops, trail on structure, and remove bid-ask from your chain — you eliminate roughly 80% of the emotional mistakes most traders make. These techniques also help you develop a healthier relationship with losses and setbacks, which is crucial for long-term success as covered in our guide on dealing with trading losses.

You don't need more indicators. You don't need more setups. You need the discipline and conviction to hold your trades for the bigger move. And that can only happen when fewer things are triggering your emotions.

The Four-Rule Summary

  • Hide your P&L — Stops emotional exits based on dollar amounts
  • Break-even runner system — Lets you hold with zero risk after first profit
  • Trail stop on stock structure — Catches bigger trend moves instead of scalping
  • Remove bid-ask display — Eliminates premium-watching and panic selling

Do these four things consistently, every single day, and I promise your results will change dramatically.
