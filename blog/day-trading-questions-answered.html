Day Trading Questions Answered: Your Biggest Trading Problems, Solved

By Somesh | Kay Capitals University

I get hundreds of trading questions every week on Instagram, Discord, and YouTube. Most of them come down to the same handful of problems — bad entries, holding losers, fear of losing, no strategy, and not knowing when to exit. If you're new to trading, start with our stock market basics guide to build a foundation.

So I sat down and answered them all in one session. I'm Somesh, full-time day trader and founder of Kay Capitals University, and this is your day trading FAQ — the real answers, no sugar coating. For specific help with fear-based issues, check out our guide on overcoming fear in trading.

"How Do I Hold My Winners?"

This is the number one question I get, and it always comes back to the same root cause: you don't have a strategy that tells you when to exit.

If you're holding because someone else told you to buy, you have no exit plan. You don't know when to get out because you didn't know why you got in.

The fix: You need a strategy with clear rules. Entry. Stop loss. Profit target. If the trade is working — structure is intact, higher highs and higher lows on your time frame — you hold. If structure breaks, you exit. No opinions. No hope. Just rules.

Negative risk management is how I specifically hold winners: take 50% at the first target, move stop to break even, and let the runner ride until structure breaks. For more detailed strategies on this topic, read our comprehensive profit taking guide.

"How Do I Minimize Fakeouts?"

If you're getting caught in fakeouts, you're probably trading breakouts without waiting for consolidation.

There are two types of breakouts:

  1. Price approaches a level and breaks immediately — This is usually a false breakout. No consolidation. Premiums are expensive. Stop loss is wide. Avoid these.
  2. Price approaches, consolidates for one or two 5-minute candles, then breaks — This is the higher probability play. Consolidation cools down premiums and gives you a tighter stop loss.

My personal opinion: about 65% of breakouts fail. The only ones I trade are breakout-and-retest setups, where price breaks a level, pulls back to it, and I enter on the retest. Much better risk-to-reward.

"What Are Your Highest Probability Setups?"

My best setup is a pullback entry on a trending day.

Here's how it works: in the first 15-20 minutes, I identify a strong trend (big range candles with volume). Then I wait for the stock to pull back to key EMAs — like the 8 EMA or VWAP — on a 5-minute chart. When it bounces off the EMA, that's my entry.

Options are cheapest at pullbacks. That's when I size up. That's when the risk-to-reward is best. I'm not chasing breakouts — I'm buying dips in a trend.

"How Do I Move My Stop Loss Up While in Profit?"

Here's the framework:

  1. Enter the trade. Stop loss at your predetermined level.
  2. Price makes a big move. Bring stop loss to break even.
  3. Price consolidates, then breaks out again. Move stop loss up to the consolidation low.
  4. Repeat with every new consolidation breakout.

Each time price consolidates and breaks higher, you ratchet your stop up. This way, even if the trade reverses, you walk away green.

"Which Time Frame Should I Use for Support and Resistance?"

Daily chart — for major, critical levels only. The big zones that everybody sees.

60-minute chart (with extended hours turned on) — for intraday levels.

That's it. Don't draw levels on the 30, 15, 5, and 1-minute charts. You'll end up with so many lines that you can't take any trade because there's always a "level" in the way. Simplify.

"How Do I Deal With Fear of Losing?"

Let's be honest about something: fear of losing is tied to your life situation.

A single parent with bills and a mortgage is going to feel more fear than an 18-year-old living at home with no expenses. That's not weakness — that's reality.

The practical fix is two things:

  1. Trade with money you can afford to lose. If every trade feels like life or death, your position size is too big for your situation.
  2. Build your back-testing statistics. When you've seen a setup work 60 out of 100 times with a 2:1 reward ratio, you stop fearing individual losses because you know the math works in your favor over time.

Fear never fully goes away. And it shouldn't — a healthy amount of fear keeps you from doing reckless things. The goal is to manage it, not eliminate it.

"What Daily Habits Make a Profitable Trader?"

  1. Sleep — Find your sweet spot. You need to know your number.
  2. Meditation — 15-20 minutes every morning. Phones off. Just breathe and clear the noise.
  3. Cold showers — Hands down the best discipline hack I've ever adopted.
  4. Back-testing/journaling — Every. Single. Day. This isn't a one-time thing.
  5. Discipline outside trading — Eat clean. Show up on time. Don't let yourself slide in other areas of life and expect to be disciplined at 9:30 AM.

"How Do I Come Back After Red Days?"

Don't try to make the money back. Seriously. The money from those losses doesn't exist anymore. Chasing a number that's gone leads you nowhere.

Instead, focus on one thing: break the losing chain.

Come in with tiny size. Make $20. Close your broker. Chain broken. The next day, your entire psychology resets. You're no longer "in a losing streak" — you're starting fresh.

I once had a 13-day losing streak. Thirteen consecutive red days. I broke the chain with a $90 green day. Walked away immediately. The very next day, I made back everything I'd lost in those 13 days — in a single session.

The money comes back when you stop chasing it.

"Best Way to Learn Price Action?"

Watch live trading. Not edited highlight reels where everything looks perfect. Watch someone trade in real time — see the losses, the missed entries, the adjustments. That's where the real learning happens.

The traders with the best content are usually the ones who can't be bothered with fancy editing. Their videos might be boring, but they're real. Find those people. Or better yet, trade alongside someone live every day.
