The Ultimate Guide to Profit Taking: When to Take Profits Day Trading

By Somesh | Kay Capitals University

You enter a trade. It starts going your way. You're up $400. Then it pulls back 20 cents and you panic. You close the position. Five minutes later, the stock rockets to your original target and you've left hundreds — maybe thousands — on the table.

This is the profit-taking problem, and it plagues nearly every day trader. Knowing when to take profits day trading is just as important as knowing when to enter. Maybe more important.

I'm Somesh, founder of Kay Capitals University. I struggled with this for a long time. But I figured out a profit taking strategy that works — and I'm going to give it to you right now.

Why Most Traders Can't Hold Winners

Let's be honest about what's happening. You enter a position. It moves in your favor. Your P&L shows green. Your brain starts doing math: "I'm up $400... that's groceries for the week... what if it comes back?"

Then it pulls back slightly — totally normal price action — and you see that $400 become $250. Your brain panics. "I was up $400! I can't lose this!" So you exit.

The trade continues to your target. You made $250 when you should have made $800. And now you feel worse than if you'd taken a loss, because you knew you were right and still didn't capitalize.

The root cause? You're watching your P&L instead of watching the chart.

Strategy #1: Hide Your P&L

This is the most counterintuitive piece of advice you'll hear, and it's the most effective.

Delete the P&L tab from your broker. If you use Interactive Brokers like I do, remove the panel that shows your unrealized gains and losses.

Why? Because if you don't know you were up $400, you can't panic when it drops to $250. You're just watching price action, trading your levels, and letting the trade play out.

This single change helped me hold positions significantly longer. When you remove the emotional trigger — seeing the dollar amount — you trade the chart instead of trading your feelings.

Strategy #2: Scale Out and Move Stop to Breakeven

Here's the mechanical profit taking strategy that I teach inside Kay Capitals University:

  1. Enter your full position (let's say 10 contracts at $1.20)
  2. Take partial profits when price hits your first target (sell 5 contracts at $1.50 — that's a 25% gain)
  3. Move your stop loss to breakeven on the remaining contracts

Now think about what you've done. You've locked in profit on half the position. And the other half? It's risk-free. If the stock reverses and hits your stop, you get out at breakeven on the remaining contracts. You've already paid yourself on the first batch.

But if the stock keeps running? You ride that second half to your extended target and make serious money.

This approach solves the biggest problem traders have: it lets you be right AND get paid for being right.

Strategy #3: Trail Your Stop Using Higher Highs and Higher Lows

This is the advanced move — and it's where the big money lives.

If you're in a long position and the stock is trending up (higher highs, higher lows), keep moving your stop loss up to each new higher low.

Here's what this does:

  • You never give back too much profit
  • You stay in the trade as long as the trend is intact
  • You let the market tell you when the move is over

On trending days, this strategy can double or triple your profits compared to taking a fixed target. And on choppy days? Your breakeven stop from Strategy #2 protects you.

The combination is powerful: scale out at your first target, trail the remainder using swing structure. This is how professional traders handle profit taking, and it's a core part of the KCU curriculum.

Strategy #4: Delete Bid/Ask from Your Option Chain

This one is specifically for options trading, and it's a game-changer.

Go into your option chain settings and remove the bid and ask prices. Keep only volume, open interest, and the strike price.

Why? Same principle as hiding your P&L. If you can't see what your contracts are currently trading at, you can't make emotional decisions based on unrealized gains. You're forced to trade based on the stock price and your predefined levels — which is exactly what you should be doing.

Why This Matters Even More for Small Accounts

If you're growing a small account, profit taking strategy isn't optional — it's survival.

Let's say you have a $300 position and you catch a 10% move. That's $30. After commissions, platform fees, and data subscriptions, that $30 might be $15. Is that worth the risk you took?

Small accounts NEED to catch bigger moves to stay profitable. You can't afford to panic-sell at every pullback. The strategies above — hiding P&L, scaling out, trailing stops — are specifically designed to help you hold longer and capture more of the move.

The Profit Taking Framework — Summary

  1. Hide your P&L — Remove the emotional trigger
  2. Scale out + breakeven stop — Lock in partial profits, ride the rest risk-free
  3. Trail stops using chart structure — Let trending days pay you big
  4. Remove bid/ask from option chain — Force yourself to trade the chart, not the premium

Stick to this system for 30 days. Your results will speak for themselves.
