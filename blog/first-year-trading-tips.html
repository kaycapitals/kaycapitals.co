6 First Year Trading Tips I Wish Someone Told Me Before I Lost Thousands

By Somesh | Kay Capitals University

If you're in your first year of trading, listen up. I'm about to save you months of pain, blown accounts, and sleepless nights. These are the six new trader tips I learned the hard way — through sweat, losses, and years of grinding charts. I'm Somesh, full-time day trader and founder of Kay Capitals University, and I genuinely wish someone had beaten these lessons into my head when I was starting out.

The thing is, plenty of people told me this stuff early on. I heard it. I nodded. And then I ignored every single word. So if you're a complete beginner reading this — don't be me. Write these down. These first year trading tips are the foundation of everything.

1. Back Test Your Strategies — This Is Non-Negotiable

This is the single most important piece of beginner day trader advice I can give you: back test your strategies.

I don't care how exciting a setup looks. I don't care if your buddy made $5K on it yesterday. If you haven't back tested it, you're gambling — not trading.

Here's what back testing actually looks like. You open up an Excel sheet. You create columns: date, ticker, bounce or reject, open price, key level, moving averages, links to charts, and comments. Then you go back through historical data and log every instance of your setup. Over time, you build a database of how many times this setup actually works.

Now when that setup appears in real time, you're not guessing. You have statistics backing you up. You've seen this pattern dozens, maybe hundreds of times. That confidence changes everything.

At Kay Capitals University, this is one of the first things we drill into students. Back testing is the difference between someone who trades for a few months and blows up, and someone who builds a career out of day trading.

2. Only Trade Options With Enough Volume

This is a mistake I see new traders make constantly: they jump into option contracts with terrible volume and then wonder why they got destroyed on the spread.

Here's my rule. In the first 15 minutes of market open, I want to see at least 1,500 volume on the contract I'm trading. By 10:00 AM, I want at least 3,000. If it's below that? I'm either sizing down dramatically or I'm not touching it at all.

Why does this matter? Because if there's not enough liquidity, the bid-ask spread gets wide. That means you're taking a hit before the trade even moves. You could be right on direction and still lose money because you didn't respect the volume.

The names I trade — SPY, QQQ, Apple, AMD, Amazon, Nvidia, Microsoft, Tesla — they have a lot of volume overall. But not every strike price has enough. You need to be selective.

3. Be Disciplined — Follow Your Trading Plan

Man, let me tell you a secret: if you want to succeed as a trader, you must follow your trading plan. Period.

Every single day you should have a plan. "If this stock goes above this price, I'm getting in. If it doesn't break this level, I'm not jumping in." That simple.

And here's where the back testing connects: you're not going to take any trades that aren't in your back test. What happens? All of a sudden, out of all the strategies you think you know, you boil down to just the ones you've actually proven work. That's discipline built into the system.

But here's the part most people don't want to hear — you can only be disciplined in trading if you're disciplined outside of trading. If you're sitting on the couch demolishing a bag of chips every night, don't expect to magically become Mr. Discipline at 9:30 AM. Your life habits bleed into your trading habits. Fix your life, fix your trading.

4. Never Average Down on a Losing Trade

I've done it. Sometimes it worked. But way more times than I want to remember, it didn't — and those losses were catastrophic.

When you average down on options, your risk management goes straight out the window. You're doubling down on a position that's already telling you it's wrong. You're hoping, not trading.

If you want a trading career for the long run, you have to stop this immediately. You can survive a little while doing it, sure. But eventually the market will punish you for it — hard.

5. Trade Stocks That Move With the Market

When I was first starting out, I lost a lot of money trying to be a hero. The market would be ripping higher and I'd be shorting some weak stock, trying to prove a point.

Focus on relative strength and relative weakness. If SPY is going up and Apple is going up, you have a much higher probability trade going long on Apple. It's that simple.

For beginners, trade stocks that move with the market index. Apple tends to follow SPY. AMD follows QQQ and Nvidia. When these names are aligned with the market direction, your probability of success skyrockets.

I do trade names that diverge from the market now — but I have years of experience reading relative strength. When you're in your first year? Don't try to be fancy. Trade with the flow.

6. Understand What Overtrading Actually Means

Here's where I might lose some people — overtrading is subjective.

For some traders, taking 100 trades a day is totally fine. For others, three trades is too many. I've had days where I've taken 50 to 100 trades and been completely in the zone. Other days, two trades is my limit.

The issue isn't the number of trades. The issue is trading when things aren't working and trying to prove a point to yourself. Those are the days you'll take the biggest hits. When the market isn't giving you what you want and you keep forcing it — that's overtrading. And overtrading never works for anybody.

Reverse-Engineer Your Success

Here's what I want you to do right now. Accept these six tips. Write them down on a piece of paper. Then reverse-engineer your trading plan from them. What do I not do? What habits do I eliminate? Start from there and work backward to where you want to go.

Trading is a learn-by-doing game. You're going to make mistakes. The goal is to make fewer of them, faster. These six first year trading tips are the shortcut I never had.
