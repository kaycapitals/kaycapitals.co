Trading Risk Management Strategies: The Rules That Keep You in the Game

By Somesh | Kay Capitals University | March 10, 2026

I'm going to say something that most trading educators won't say because it's bad for marketing: your strategy barely matters if your risk management is garbage.

I don't care if you have the greatest entry system ever created. If you're risking 10% of your account on a single trade, you're going to blow up. It's not a question of if — it's when.

I'm Somesh — Kay Capitals. I trade options on SPY, QQQ, NVDA, and TSLA every day. I've been profitable for years, and I can tell you with absolute certainty that risk management is the single most important skill I have. Not my entries. Not my analysis. My risk management. It's the backbone of everything I teach at Kay Capitals University.

Let me give you the exact trading risk management strategies I use and teach. These aren't theoretical concepts — these are rules I follow every single day.

Rule 1: The 1% Rule — Non-Negotiable

Never risk more than 1% of your trading account on a single trade. Period. No exceptions. No "high conviction" overrides.

If you have a $25,000 account, your maximum loss on any single trade is $250. If you have a $10,000 account, it's $100. If you have a $50,000 account, it's $500.

"But Somesh, 1% is so small. I'll never make real money."

Wrong. Here's why the 1% rule is powerful:

  • It takes a catastrophic losing streak to significantly damage your account. You'd need to lose 20+ trades in a row to draw down 20%. That's statistically near-impossible with any reasonable system.
  • It eliminates the emotional component of losing. Losing $250 on a $25,000 account doesn't trigger panic. Losing $2,500 does. And panic leads to revenge trading, which leads to more losses.
  • It lets you trade with a clear head. When the money at risk doesn't threaten your survival, you make better decisions.

I've seen it over and over with KCU students: the ones who follow the 1% rule survive long enough to become profitable. The ones who ignore it blow up within months.

Rule 2: Daily Loss Limit — 3% Max

This is where most traders go wrong. They might manage risk on individual trades but have no system for managing their day.

My rule: if I lose 3% of my account in a single day, I'm done. Laptop closed. Go for a walk. Come back tomorrow.

Three losing trades at 1% each = 3% = done for the day.

Why is this important? Because the worst trading you'll ever do is trading after consecutive losses. Your psychology shifts. You start trying to make it back. You take setups that aren't there. You size up to "recover." This is the revenge trading spiral, and it has ended more trading careers than bad strategies ever have.

The daily loss limit is your circuit breaker. It physically prevents you from doing the most damage.

  • Here's what a daily loss limit looks like in practice:
  • Trade 1: Loss (-$250) — Running daily loss: -$250
  • Trade 2: Loss (-$200) — Running daily loss: -$450
  • Trade 3: Loss (-$220) — Running daily loss: -$670, approaching 3% on a $25K account
  • Result: Done for the day. No more trades. Accept the loss. Tomorrow is a new day.

Without this rule, Trade 4 becomes a double-size "revenge" trade, Trade 5 becomes a desperation play on TSLA earnings, and by Trade 8 you've lost 10% of your account. I've seen it a thousand times.

Rule 3: Position Sizing — Calculate Before You Click

Most beginners decide their position size based on vibes. "I'll buy 5 contracts." Why 5? "It felt right." That's not a system. That's gambling.

Here's how you actually calculate position size:

  1. Determine your max risk (1% of account)
  2. Determine your stop loss in dollar terms per contract
  3. Divide max risk by per-contract risk = number of contracts
  • Example:
  • Account: $25,000
  • Max risk per trade: $250 (1%)
  • You want to buy SPY calls at $2.50
  • Your stop loss is at $1.50 (you'll lose $1.00 per contract = $100 per contract)
  • Position size: $250 / $100 = 2.5, round down to 2 contracts

This is mechanical. No emotion. No "this one feels like a banger so I'll load up." Every trade is sized the same way, based on your risk tolerance, not your feelings.

Rule 4: Stop Losses Are Not Optional

I hear every excuse in the book for not using stop losses:

"Market makers hunt stop losses." (Mostly myth at retail sizes.) "I'll just watch it and exit manually." (You won't. Your emotions will freeze you.) "The spread is too wide for stops." (Then you're trading the wrong instrument.)

Here's the reality: a stop loss is the only thing standing between a small, manageable loss and a catastrophic one. On 0DTE SPY options, a move against you can turn a $200 loss into an $800 loss in minutes. By the time you "decide" to get out, the damage is done.

  • Types of stop losses I use:
  • Hard stop: A literal order in the system that executes automatically. Set it and forget it.
  • Mental stop with rules: I have the price level decided before entry, and I execute within 5 seconds of it being hit. This requires discipline and is for advanced traders only.
  • Time stop: If the trade hasn't moved in my favor within 15-30 minutes, I reduce or exit. Not every losing trade hits your price stop — sometimes the trade just isn't working.

The best stop loss is the one you actually use. Start with hard stops. Graduate to mental stops once you've proven to yourself (through journaling data, not feelings) that you can execute them consistently.

Rule 5: Risk-to-Reward — Minimum 1:2

If you're risking $250 to make $150, you need a 63% win rate just to break even. That's hard to maintain consistently.

If you're risking $250 to make $500, you only need a 34% win rate to break even. That's dramatically more achievable.

Every trade I take has a minimum 1:2 risk-to-reward ratio. I'm risking $1 to make at least $2. On the best setups, I'm looking at 1:3 or even 1:4.

This means I'm wrong on almost half my trades and still profitable. Read that again. I don't need to be right most of the time. I just need my wins to be bigger than my losses. That's the mathematical edge that consistency is built on.

  • How to set risk-to-reward:
  • Before entering, identify your entry, stop loss, and target
  • Calculate the distance from entry to stop (your risk)
  • Calculate the distance from entry to target (your reward)
  • If reward isn't at least 2x risk, skip the trade

Simple. Not easy to follow when you're staring at a moving chart, but simple.

Rule 6: Weekly Loss Limit — 6%

Beyond the daily limit, I have a weekly loss limit. If I'm down 6% for the week, I stop trading for the remainder of the week.

This catches situations where you have multiple max-loss days in a row. Three days of hitting your 3% daily limit is 9% of your account — that's significant and signals something is off. Either the market conditions don't suit your system, or your execution is slipping.

The weekly limit forces you to step back, review what's happening, and come back fresh the following week. It prevents the death-by-a-thousand-cuts drawdown that can slowly drain an account over weeks of mediocre trading.

Rule 7: Scale Your Risk to Your Skill

When you're new, 1% per trade might even be too aggressive. I start KCU students at 0.5% per trade for the first 30 days. Half percent. It sounds like nothing, but it serves two critical purposes:

  1. It keeps your losses tiny while you're building foundational skills
  2. It teaches you that position size doesn't correlate with skill. Your skill shows in your consistency, not your size.

You earn the right to size up by proving you can follow the rules consistently. My rule for students: 30 consecutive trading days of following all risk rules → you can move from 0.5% to 1%. 60 days → you can consider 1.5% on A+ setups.

This progressive risk model is something I've never seen taught elsewhere, and it's one of the things that makes the biggest difference in student results at KCU.

Rule 8: No Averaging Down on Day Trades

This rule has saved my ass more times than I can count. When a trade goes against me, I do not add to it. Ever. Not on day trades.

"But the price is even better now!" No. The trade went against you. Your thesis was either wrong or your timing was off. Adding to a loser is how you turn a small loss into a portfolio-altering event.

Averaging down works in long-term investing. In day trading options with expiring contracts, it's financial suicide. Your 0DTE SPY put doesn't care about your average cost — if the market keeps running against you, more contracts just means more losses.

One entry. One stop. One outcome. That's the discipline.

Rule 9: Separate Your Trading Capital From Your Life

This isn't a "strategy" in the traditional sense, but it's crucial risk management for your overall financial health.

Your trading account should be money you can afford to lose without affecting your rent, bills, food, or emergency fund. If losing your trading capital would create a personal financial crisis, you are not in a position to trade effectively because the pressure will compromise every decision you make.

I tell prospective KCU students this directly: don't join the program with your last $3,000 and don't fund your trading account with money you need for bills. Get your financial house in order first, then trade with capital that's dedicated to this purpose.

Rule 10: Review, Adapt, Improve

Risk management isn't static. As your account grows, your dollar risk per trade changes. As market conditions shift, certain strategies need adjustment. As you develop as a trader, your risk framework should evolve with you.

  • Every month, review your risk metrics:
  • What was your average risk per trade?
  • Did you follow your daily loss limit every day?
  • What was your largest drawdown and why?
  • Were there days you broke your rules? What triggered it?

This monthly review is where you catch drift. Where rules slowly erode. Where you unconsciously started sizing up because you were on a hot streak. The data keeps you honest.

Why This Matters More Than Your Strategy

I could give you the best options trading strategy in the world right now. Perfect entries, perfect exits. And without proper risk management, you would still find a way to lose money. Because one bad day where you ignore your rules can erase weeks of good trading.

Risk management is the foundation. It's not sexy. It doesn't get Instagram engagement. Nobody screenshots their stop loss execution and posts "look at this beautiful risk management." But it's the thing that keeps you in the game long enough for your strategy to work.

Master these rules. Follow them religiously. And you'll already be ahead of 80% of traders who blow up not because they're dumb, but because they refuse to manage risk.
